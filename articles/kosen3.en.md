# Putting propulsion on a small satellite

In the KOSEN-3 CubeSat Launch Project, I worked on the development of a pulsed plasma thruster for a CubeSat. In photos it may look like a collection of metal parts and wires, but the real challenge was fitting meaningful propulsion into strict limits of volume, power, and mass.

![Thruster experiment setup](images/kosen3_1.jpg)
*During experiments, I adjusted conditions step by step while watching how the setup behaved.*

## What I Worked On

My main work involved studying thrust improvement with a magnetic nozzle, organizing experimental data, and analyzing results with Python. Research was not a one-shot measurement. It required checking conditions, noise, repeatability, and the character of the equipment before updating the hypothesis.

- Performance evaluation of a pulsed plasma thruster
- Thrust improvement using magnetic fields
- Data analysis in Python
- Interactive presentation material with React and TypeScript
- Conference presentations and paper writing

![Research presentation and materials](images/kosen3_2.jpg)
*Research is not only about graphs and equations. It also has to be communicated clearly.*

## What I Learned

This project trained me to handle ambiguous phenomena. Theoretical expectations and experimental values do not naturally line up. I had to separate possible causes across the device, measurement process, analysis, and hypothesis.

> Finding an interesting phenomenon is one thing. Explaining it in a repeatable way is much harder.

That mindset still helps me in software development: reading logs, controlling conditions, and testing small hypotheses. The practical habits I learned in research now show up directly in implementation and debugging.
