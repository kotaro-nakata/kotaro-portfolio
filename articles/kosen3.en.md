# Giving a small satellite the option of propulsion

The [KOSEN-3 Project](https://www.akashi.ac.jp/news/2022/20230209sou001.html) is a project to develop and launch the KOSEN-3 microsatellite through a collaboration among eight KOSEN colleges and two universities, led mainly by Kagawa KOSEN and Akashi KOSEN. It was selected as one of the demonstration themes for JAXA's Innovative Satellite Technology Demonstration-5 and is scheduled to be launched. I joined from the early stage as a member of one theme: demonstrating technology to extend orbital lifetime using a PPT (Pulsed Plasma Thruster) propulsion system.

## What I Worked On

My main responsibilities were studying thrust improvement for the PPT, developing an ultra-small thrust measurement device, and organizing and analyzing experimental data. A PPT is an electric propulsion system that uses plasma, so I researched ways to improve thrust using magnetic fields. The hardest part was developing the ultra-small thrust measurement device. The force produced by one pulse of this PPT is weaker than a breath from your nose. There was no device I could simply buy to measure that level of thrust, so I built one myself.

## What I Learned

Through experiments at JAXA and two years of research, I learned a tremendous amount. I also challenged myself with things I had not learned in class. Even though I was in the electrical engineering department, I had to do work that felt closer to mechanical engineering, and there were periods when I went to the school workshop every day.
One lesson that especially stayed with me was to "write everything down." In research, small flashes of insight, discoveries, and comments can become the driving force behind major progress. During experiments, writing down every detail helped me identify causes when accidents or problems occurred.
